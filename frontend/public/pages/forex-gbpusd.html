GBP/USD

The Cable. The volatility king of the majors. EUR/USD's louder, faster, more emotional cousin. Bigger pip ranges, deeper sweeps, brutal fakeouts - and when you read it right, the kind of moves that make a week in one afternoon. Welcome to London's playground.

~12 min read Major Pairs Follows EUR/USD
TL;DR - the pair in one paragraph

GBP/USD is EUR/USD on espresso. 30-50% wider daily ranges, 2-3x bigger London Open sweeps, deeper retracements, and a tendency to fake out one direction before going the other way. London session belongs to GBP - that's when the real money is moving sterling. Spread is wider than EUR/USD (0.3-0.6 pip raw vs 0.0-0.3), so you pay for the volatility. Master this pair second, after EUR/USD feels boring.

Where the name "Cable" comes from

Quick history detour because it's actually a great story. In the 1860s, before satellites and fiber optics, the only way to send a price quote between London and New York was through a literal transatlantic telegraph cable laid across the Atlantic seabed. The first successful cable went live in 1866, connecting Valentia Bay in Ireland to Heart's Content in Newfoundland.

Forex traders in both cities started referring to the GBP/USD exchange rate as "the cable rate" - the rate that came through the cable. The name stuck. Today, 160 years later, every forex desk on earth still calls GBP/USD "Cable". Bloomberg ticker still calls it that. It's the only major with a permanent nickname.

If you hear a trader say "long Cable at 1.27" - they mean GBP/USD. If you say "long GBP" they'll assume you mean GBP against the dollar unless you specify a cross. Cable = GBP/USD, always.

Why GBP/USD moves harder

Three structural reasons GBP/USD has bigger ranges than EUR/USD even though both have major economies behind them.

One - smaller market depth. EUR/USD has 24% of global forex volume. GBP/USD has roughly 9%. That's still huge in absolute terms, but it's 2.5x less liquidity than EUR/USD. Less liquidity = wider moves on the same volume of orders. A $5B hedge fund position in EUR/USD barely moves the needle. The same position in GBP/USD shifts the pair 20-30 pips.

Two - the UK economy is smaller and more concentrated. The Eurozone is 19 countries with diversified industries. The UK is one country with a heavy weighting in financial services (which makes GBP sensitive to risk-on/risk-off) and a still-recovering relationship with its biggest trading partner (the EU). Brexit isn't 2016 anymore, but its aftershocks still appear in trade data, Northern Ireland disputes, and the occasional political crisis. All of that translates to wider sterling moves.

Three - the BoE is more reactive. The ECB is famously slow to move - 25 basis points at a time, well telegraphed, rarely surprises markets. The Bank of England has historically been more willing to surprise: emergency hikes, larger-than-expected cuts, hawkish-then-dovish pivots within a single quarter. That unpredictability translates directly to GBP volatility around BoE meetings.

The fundamentals - BoE vs Fed

Same structural game as EUR/USD - two central banks pulling on a rope - but the players have different personalities:

  • The Bank of England (BoE) - sets the bank rate, runs QT/QE cycles, releases the MPC (Monetary Policy Committee) decision and minutes 8 times a year. Vote splits matter - a 5-4 hike feels less hawkish than a 9-0 hike even if the rate change is identical.
  • The US Federal Reserve (Fed) - same role as for EUR/USD. The Fed's dot plot and FOMC statement also move GBP/USD because the USD side of the pair is identical.

Watch the rate differential the same way you watch it for EUR/USD. But for GBP, also watch UK CPI specifically - the BoE is more inflation-reactive than the ECB historically.

That spread difference matters. On a 10 pip intraday move, an extra 0.3 pip spread is 3% drag. On a scalp strategy with 50+ trades per month, that 3% adds up to real money. The volatility compensates - but only if you actually catch the moves.

London Open - GBP's home turf

If EUR/USD's best session is the London-NY overlap, GBP/USD's is the London Open (02:00-05:00 NY, 07:00-10:00 London). This is when sterling does most of its damage.

Reason: GBP is London's currency. The UK gilts market opens, UK equities open, UK corporate flows fire. EUR/USD also reacts to London open but the move on GBP is bigger because GBP is the local currency. Combine that with the typical London Open liquidity sweep pattern (sweep Asian high or low, reverse) and you get the cleanest, biggest setups of the GBP trading week between 02:00 and 05:00 NY.

The Cable's signature trick - the double sweep

EUR/USD usually sweeps one side of the Asian range at London Open and reverses. GBP/USD likes to do something more annoying: sweep one side first, reverse, then sweep the other side, then reverse for real.

This double-sweep pattern catches everyone. The first sweep grabs the early longs (or shorts). The reversal looks legit, so traders jump in. Then GBP rips back through the other side, stops out the new positions, and then finally moves in the eventual direction. Three groups of retail get murdered before the actual setup forms.

Real-world example of a double sweep

03:00 NY. Asian range: 1.2710 - 1.2735.

03:15 - GBP/USD sweeps the Asian high, hits 1.2745. Looks like a sweep-and-reverse for shorts.

03:30 - reverses down, breaks back below the Asian range. Shorts feel smart.

04:00 - dives all the way to 1.2695, sweeping the Asian low. Stops out the early shorts who entered at 1.2740 with SL above 1.2750.

04:15 - bottoms at 1.2695, forms a 15m MSS up, retests an FVG at 1.2708, and rips up to 1.2755 by 06:00.

The "real" setup was the second sweep + MSS. Anyone who entered on the first sweep got stopped out. Welcome to the Cable.

On GBP/USD, the second sweep is the setup. The first sweep is bait. Wait for the double.

Key levels - what GBP/USD actually respects

Mostly the same hierarchy as EUR/USD, but with different reliability:

  1. Previous Day's High / Low (PDH / PDL) - GBP sweeps PDH or PDL on ~70% of trading days (higher than EUR/USD's 65%). Sweep-and-reverse plays here are gold.
  2. Previous Week's High / Low (PWH / PWL) - very respected, often gives 80-120 pip reactions on first test.
  3. New Week Opening Gap (NWOG) - usually fills within Monday-Tuesday on GBP.
  4. Round numbers (1.2700, 1.2750, 1.2800) - magnetic on GBP. Round-number stop hunts are a daily occurrence.
  5. The "00" and "50" levels specifically - GBP loves to sweep 1.2700, sweep 1.2750, sweep 1.2800. Mark these for every trading session.

One difference from EUR/USD: GBP/USD is more likely to break and reverse at key levels rather than rejecting cleanly. A clean rejection on EUR/USD might be a wick + 1 confirming candle. On GBP, the rejection often happens after a 15-25 pip false break.

ICT/SMC on GBP/USD

The concepts work, but the parameters change:

  • Liquidity sweeps - sweeps are bigger and faster. Expect 30-60 pip sweeps where you'd see 15-30 on EUR/USD. Your invalidation level needs to account for that.
  • MSS - market structure shifts on GBP often happen on 5m and 15m TFs but get faked on 1m. Stick to 5m+ for confirmation.
  • FVGs - 1H and 4H FVGs are very reliable. 5m FVGs are noisy because the bigger pip ranges make small FVGs less meaningful. Use 15m as the floor for entry TF.
  • Order Blocks - work well but the OB body needs to be larger. A 5-pip OB on GBP is basically nothing - look for OB candles that are 15+ pips with body.
  • Killzones - London Open killzone (02:00-05:00 NY) is GBP's prime time. NY AM still works but the move is usually 60-70% of the London Open move's magnitude.

Two things to flag: UK political risk is real. Snap elections, no-confidence votes, Northern Ireland Protocol disputes - these can move GBP 100+ pips on no economic news at all. EUR/USD doesn't have this in the same way because the Eurozone has no single political center.

Also BoE communication is critical. The MPC meeting itself often produces a 30 pip move; the press conference an hour later sometimes triples that. Don't be in a position 30 minutes before the conference.

The GBP/JPY connection - your sister pair

One quirk specific to GBP traders: GBP/JPY is highly correlated with GBP/USD, but bigger. GBP/JPY is roughly the "stress amplifier" of sterling - moves go further, faster, and with more conviction.

Practical implication: if you can read the Cable, you can almost trade GBP/JPY blind in the same direction (with smaller position size because the pip values are different). And vice versa - GBP/JPY sometimes leads. If GBP/JPY breaks structure first, GBP/USD often follows within minutes.

This isn't a setup recommendation - just an awareness piece. Keep GBP/JPY on a secondary chart when you're in a Cable trade. If GBP/JPY breaks against you, your Cable trade is probably wrong.

Bread-and-butter setups on GBP/USD

Setup 1 - London Open Double Sweep

The bread and butter. Pre-market mark the Asian range (20:00-02:00 NY). At London Open, wait. Don't enter on the first sweep. Wait for the price to come back, sweep the other side, and only then look for MSS and entry.

Setup mechanics

Asian range: 1.2710-1.2735. Bias from H4: bullish.

03:15 NY - sweep the Asian high to 1.2745, reverse down. Don't short.

04:00 NY - sweep the Asian low to 1.2695. Now look for 15m bullish MSS.

04:20 NY - 15m MSS prints, retest 15m FVG at 1.2705-1.2710. Long, SL below 1.2695 (below the second sweep wick). Target: 1.2755-1.2780.

Typical RR: 3-6R.

Setup 2 - BoE Decision Day Fade

BoE typically releases the rate decision at 07:00 NY (12:00 UK). The initial spike is often the wrong direction - especially on close vote splits or "dovish hike" / "hawkish hold" scenarios. Don't trade the spike. Wait 15-30 minutes, let price establish a real direction, then look for a 5m MSS in the corrected direction and enter on the first pullback.

Setup mechanics

BoE expected to hold rates at 5.25%. Markets priced 30% chance of cut.

07:00 NY - BoE holds, but vote split is 6-3 in favor of a cut (more dovish than expected).

07:00-07:05 - GBP spikes up 40 pips on relief rally, then stalls.

07:05-07:30 - reversal back down through the pre-release level, MSS prints on 5m.

07:35 - retest of the 5m FVG inside the reversal leg. Short, SL above the post-release high. Target: pre-release range low or PDL.

Typical RR: 2-4R. Don't take the initial spike trade. Wait for the correction.

Risk profile - sizing for a bigger pair

Typical intraday SL on GBP/USD: 20-45 pips. That's double EUR/USD. Which means - if you're using 1% risk on a $5,000 account - your position size on GBP needs to be roughly half of what you'd use on EUR/USD for the same dollar risk.

This is the most common mistake on Cable: traders carry the same lot size habits from EUR/USD and get smoked because the bigger SL means bigger dollar risk per trade. Use the Lot Sizing formula mechanically, not by feel.

Sizing comparison

$5,000 account, 1% risk = $50 per trade.

EUR/USD setup, 20 pip SL: lot size = $50 / (20 × $10) = 0.25 lot

GBP/USD setup, 40 pip SL: lot size = $50 / (40 × $10) = 0.125 lot

Same dollar risk. Half the position on GBP. Most retail traders do not adjust and end up risking 2% on every GBP trade by accident.

When not to trade GBP/USD

  • 30 minutes before BoE or FOMC - spread doubles, stops slippage, get murdered. Be flat.
  • Asian session - even quieter than EUR/USD's Asian session. Cable in Tokyo hours is a coin flip with cost.
  • Friday afternoon - GBP gets thin into the weekend, sweeps are random rather than directional.
  • UK bank holidays (and there are more than you'd expect) - London desks are out, GBP liquidity drops, fake-outs multiply.
  • During UK political shock days - "snap election called", "PM resignation" - GBP becomes a headline machine. Skip the session.
  • When you're tilted from a EUR/USD loss - revenge-trading EUR loss on GBP is the fastest way to double down on a bad day.

Common mistakes on GBP/USD

  1. Carrying EUR/USD lot sizing onto GBP - the bigger SL means you're risking 1.5-2x per trade without realizing it.
  2. Entering on the first London Open sweep - on GBP it's almost always bait. Wait for the second sweep.
  3. Tight SLs because "tight SLs are professional" - GBP's natural noise will hit any SL under 15 pips. Either use 20+ pip SLs or trade a different pair.
  4. Trading BoE press conferences - the spreads, slippage, and headline whipsaw are not worth the move. Wait until 30 minutes after.
  5. Confusing GBP volatility with opportunity - just because Cable is moving doesn't mean there's a setup. No setup, no trade, no matter how much it's running.

Last words - respect the Cable

GBP/USD will reward you for patience and punish you for arrogance. The pair has murdered more accounts than any other forex major because traders see the bigger pip ranges and think "more pips = more profit". Wrong. More pips means more opportunity - and more risk. Without adjusted sizing and a different setup discipline (wait for the double sweep, use wider SLs, scale into BoE days), Cable will eat you alive.

If you've got 3 months of solid EUR/USD trading behind you, you're ready for GBP. Same playbook, just dial up your patience by 30%, your SL by 50%, and your sizing by 0.5x. The wins are bigger, the losses are bigger, but the edge is the same.
